Pigments & the Rainbow

Egyptian artisans

c. 1500 BCE
Egyptian glassmakers produced coloured pigments and treated the rainbow as a phenomenon worthy of record — humanity's earliest engagement with the decomposition of colour.[1]

Reflection in Water Vessels

Thales of Miletus

c. 585 BCE
Thales is credited with the earliest Greek investigations of reflection and magnification through water-filled vessels, beginning a tradition of treating light as a subject of study.[2]

Pinhole Camera

Mo Zi & the Mohists

c. 400 BCE
The Mojing describes pinhole cameras, image inversion, and the rectilinear propagation of light — arguably the world's first systematic optics.[3]

Atmospheric Refraction

Cleomedes

c. 50 CE
Cleomedes describes the bending of light entering the atmosphere and uses it to explain why the Sun remains visible when geometrically below the horizon.[4]

Tables of Refraction

Claudius Ptolemy

c. 150 CE
Ptolemy's Optics contains the first known quantitative tables of refraction angles for air–water and air–glass — a precursor to Snell's law.[5]

Theory of the Rainbow

Roger Bacon

c. 1267
In the Opus Majus, Bacon analyses the rainbow as the product of refraction within droplets and insists on experiment as the route to natural knowledge.[6]

The Visible Spectrum

Isaac Newton

1666
Newton's prism — and the second prism that recombined the colours — showed that white light is a heterogeneous mixture of rays. He coined the word spectrum.[7]

See Theory of the Rainbow

Dark Lines in Sunlight

William Hyde Wollaston

1802
Using a narrow slit rather than a round aperture, Wollaston was the first to see dark lines crossing the solar spectrum — though he mistook them for natural boundaries between colours.[8]

See The Diffraction Grating

The Diffraction Grating

Joseph von Fraunhofer

1814
Fraunhofer mapped 574 dark lines in the solar spectrum with unprecedented precision and built ruled gratings that turned spectroscopy into a quantitative science.[9]

See Dark Lines in Sunlight · Spectral Analysis of Elements

Spectral Analysis of Elements

Kirchhoff & Bunsen

1859
Each chemical element emits and absorbs at a characteristic set of wavelengths. Kirchhoff and Bunsen turned this into the founding principle of analytical spectroscopy and discovered caesium and rubidium by spectrum alone.[10]

See The Diffraction Grating

Balmer Formula

Johann Balmer

1885
A Swiss schoolteacher found that the visible hydrogen lines fit a simple integer formula — the first hint that atomic spectra encode discrete structure.[11]

See Rydberg Formula

Rydberg Formula

Johannes Rydberg

1888
Rydberg generalised Balmer's relation to a universal formula for the spectral lines of hydrogen-like ions, introducing the constant that still bears his name.[12]

See Balmer Formula · Quantised Atom

Quantised Atom

Niels Bohr

1913
Bohr's quantised model of the atom derived the Rydberg formula from first principles, binding spectroscopy to the emerging quantum theory.[13]

See Rydberg Formula · Balmer Formula

Zeeman Effect

Pieter Zeeman

1896
Sodium D lines split into several components when the source sat between the poles of an electromagnet. Lorentz's classical electron theory accounted for the "normal" splitting; the "anomalous" pattern resisted explanation until the discovery of electron spin in 1925 — and Zeeman splittings later became the textbook first application of quantum-mechanical perturbation theory.[22]

See Stark Effect · Quantised Atom

Stark Effect

Johannes Stark

1913
The electric analogue of the Zeeman effect: hydrogen lines split when atoms were placed in strong electric fields. Quantitative agreement between Schrödinger's first-order perturbation calculation (1926) and Stark's measurements became one of the earliest decisive experimental tests of the new quantum mechanics.[23]

See Zeeman Effect · Quantised Atom

Raman Scattering

C. V. Raman

1928
The inelastic scattering of light by molecules opened a window onto vibrational and rotational structure — a major branch of modern spectroscopy.[14]

See Stimulated Raman Scattering

Electron Microscope

Ruska & Knoll

1931
Replacing photons with focused electron beams, Ruska and Knoll built an imaging system that broke the diffraction limit of visible light by orders of magnitude — opening the atomic-scale world to direct observation.[19]

See Electron Energy-Loss Spectroscopy

Electron Energy-Loss Spectroscopy

Hillier & Baker

1944
By measuring the energy electrons lose as they pass through a thin sample, EELS turned the transmission electron microscope into a spectroscope — mapping elemental composition and chemical bonding at sub-nanometre resolution.[20]

See Electron Microscope

Lamb Shift

Willis Lamb & Robert Retherford

1947
A 1058 MHz microwave splitting between the 2S1/2 and 2P1/2 levels of hydrogen — predicted to be exactly degenerate by Dirac — could only be explained by the atom's interaction with the fluctuating quantum vacuum. The measurement triggered Bethe's first renormalisation calculation and birthed modern quantum electrodynamics (QED).[24]

See Quantised Atom · Stark Effect

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance

Bloch & Purcell

1946
Independent groups detected resonant absorption of radio-frequency photons by nuclear spins in a magnetic field, founding NMR spectroscopy and, eventually, MRI.[15]

FRET Spectroscopy

Theodor Förster

1948
Förster predicted that an excited fluorophore can transfer energy non-radiatively to a nearby acceptor, with efficiency falling as 1/r6. FRET turned fluorescence into a molecular "ruler" for distances of 1–10 nm — now central to single-molecule biology.[21]

Stimulated Raman Scattering

Woodbury & Ng

1962
Pumping a ruby laser through nitrobenzene produced a coherent, amplified Stokes beam — the discovery of stimulated Raman scattering and the basis of coherent Raman spectroscopies (CARS, SRS microscopy).[16]

See Raman Scattering

Optical Frequency Comb

Hänsch & Hall

c. 2000
A femtosecond laser becomes a ruler for light, linking microwave atomic clocks to optical transitions and enabling spectroscopy at fractional precisions of 10−18.[17]

See Quantum Logic Spectroscopy

Quantum Logic Spectroscopy

Schmidt, Rosenband, Wineland

2005
A co-trapped "logic" ion reads out a spectroscopy ion that cannot be laser-cooled or detected directly — the technique behind today's most accurate optical clocks.[18]

See Optical Frequency Comb

Time-Dependent DFT

Runge & Gross

1984
Runge and Gross extended Kohn's density-functional theory to time-dependent external fields, providing a rigorous framework for computing optical absorption spectra, photoionisation cross-sections, and excited-state dynamics of molecules and materials from first principles. TDDFT is now the workhorse for predicting the electronic spectra of everything from dyes to catalysts.[25]

See Spectral Analysis of Elements · Quantised Atom

Coherent Control

Brumer & Shapiro; Tannor & Rice

1986
By driving an atom or molecule with two phase-locked laser fields whose interference paths to a final state can be tuned, the branching ratio between competing photochemical outcomes becomes a control parameter. The theory launched a generation of pulse-shaping experiments in chemistry, attoscience and quantum information.[26]

See Optical Frequency Comb

High-Harmonic & Attosecond Theory

Corkum; Lewenstein

c. 1993–2002
Corkum's "three-step model" (ionise, accelerate, recombine) and the Lewenstein quantum extension explained how an intense laser field can up-convert an atom's own electron into trains of attosecond XUV bursts — the theoretical foundation of attosecond spectroscopy and the 2023 Physics Nobel.[27]

See Coherent Control

Cavity Ring-Down Spectroscopy

O'Keefe & Deacon

1988
A laser pulse rattles between two ultra-high-reflectivity mirrors thousands of times; an absorber in the cavity shortens the exponential decay of the light leaking out. Measuring a decay time rather than a small intensity difference pushed absorption sensitivities below 10−9 per pass — now standard for trace-gas detection and atmospheric monitoring.[28]

See The Diffraction Grating

Single-Molecule SERS

Nie & Emory; Kneipp et al.

1997
A single molecule adsorbed on a silver-nanoparticle "hot spot" lights up by factors of 1014–1015, making its Raman fingerprint detectable one molecule at a time. Surface-enhanced Raman scattering at the single-molecule limit turned a niche analytical method into a route for label-free chemical imaging of biological systems.[29]

See Raman Scattering · Stimulated Raman Scattering

Bose-Einstein Condensation

Cornell, Wieman, Ketterle

1995
Laser cooling, evaporative cooling, and absorption-imaging spectroscopy revealed a sharp, narrow peak in the momentum distribution of trapped rubidium and sodium atoms — the matter-wave analogue of a laser. BECs are now a standard platform for studying quantum many-body physics.[30]

Antihydrogen 1S–2S

ALPHA Collaboration

2017
After trapping a few hundred atoms of antihydrogen for hundreds of seconds at CERN, ALPHA drove the 1S–2S transition with a stabilised laser and matched its frequency to ordinary hydrogen at the part-in-1012 level — the most precise spectroscopy ever performed on antimatter, and a stringent test of CPT symmetry.[31]

See Quantised Atom · Lamb Shift · Optical Frequency Comb

Helium on the Sun

Janssen & Lockyer

1868
A bright yellow line in the Sun's chromosphere (later labelled D3) did not match any known terrestrial element. Lockyer named the inferred element after the Greek hélios — 27 years before it was found on Earth. The first element ever discovered off-planet.

Stars are Mostly Hydrogen

Cecilia Payne

1925
Payne's doctoral thesis applied Saha's ionisation equation to stellar spectra and concluded that stars are overwhelmingly hydrogen and helium — a result her examiners initially called "clearly impossible," and which reshaped twentieth-century astrophysics.

An Expanding Universe

Edwin Hubble

1929
Spectral lines of distant galaxies are systematically redshifted in proportion to their distance — the empirical signature of cosmic expansion and the seed of Big Bang cosmology. Without spectroscopy there is no Hubble–Lemaître law.

Cosmic Microwave Background

Penzias & Wilson

1965
A persistent 3 K microwave hiss in a Bell Labs horn antenna — independent of direction or season — turned out to be the redshifted, near-perfect blackbody spectrum of the early universe. COBE/FIRAS later measured the spectrum to one part in 105.

MRI

Lauterbur & Mansfield

1973
Adding magnetic-field gradients to NMR turns the spin signal into a 3-D map of soft tissue. From a Nature paper in 1973 to a workhorse of clinical neurology — one of the most consequential off-spring of pure spectroscopy.

Dark Matter from Rotation Curves

Vera Rubin

c. 1978
Doppler-shifted hydrogen lines from gas in spiral galaxies showed that orbital velocities stay flat far beyond the visible disc — implying that most of each galaxy's mass is invisible "dark matter."

Protein Structures by NMR

Kurt Wüthrich

2002
Multidimensional NMR can pin down the three-dimensional fold of a protein in solution — the technique that won the 2002 Chemistry Nobel and complements X-ray crystallography across structural biology.

Water on an Exoplanet

HST & Spitzer teams

2007
When an exoplanet transits its star, a sliver of starlight passes through its atmosphere on the way to us, imprinting absorption features. Water vapour was first confirmed this way on HD 189733b — transit spectroscopy.

Gravitational Waves at LIGO

LIGO Collaboration

2015
LIGO's km-scale interferometers must keep their lasers' frequency stable to parts in 1015 — a stability borrowed directly from optical frequency-comb spectroscopy. The first detection of a binary black-hole merger was recorded on 14 September 2015.

Atomic Clocks at 10−18

NIST, JILA, RIKEN

c. 2019
Optical clocks read out by quantum-logic spectroscopy or magic-wavelength lattices now achieve fractional uncertainties below 10−18 — sensitive enough to see general-relativistic time dilation across a 1 cm change in height.
